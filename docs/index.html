    About

    These Supreme Court Predictions are made using machine learning software that has examined hundreds of past cases and learned what to look for to make a successful prediction.

    It takes several steps to arrive at a prediction for a case. First, the software goes to the Supreme Court’s website and downloads a transcript of the oral arguments. It then parses the transcript file to extract key information, like the case number, date the case was argued, and the names of the parties to the case. The software also goes through the text of the transcript itself and collects information, such as how many times each lawyer was interrupted, or how many words the justices spoke.

    Then the algorithm evaluates the case on a number of criteria: was the lawyer for the petitioners interrupted more than once every 71 words? Was there more than one lawyer for the respondents? Were there eight justices or nine? The algorithm has learned the exact values to check for each of these criteria by looking at past cases. It also knows how each criteria affects the overall chances of success for the petitioner. For example, the petitioner has won only 1.7% of cases where their lawyer was interrupted more than once every 71 words on average. The algorithm then combines all of this information into a final estimate of the chances for each side of winning the case.

    The technical name for this kind of prediction algorithm is a Naive Bayes Classifier. Because of some of the assumptions that are made by the model, the probabilities that it estimates for each side are very rough and imprecise. Other than giving some sense of the confidence of the algorithm, there's not much difference between a 56% chance and a 92% one.

    The model trains itself by looking at some past cases, while it leaves others for later so it can try to predict them without having seen them before. That way, it’s possible to get a clear sense of the model’s accuracy. Predicting over 750 cases, the model was correct 75% of the time.
